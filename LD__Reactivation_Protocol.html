Patient Retention & Reactivation Protocol

Team Operating Standard  ·  Front Office, Hygiene & Clinical
  • The cheapest patient to keep is the one already in the chair.
  • Reactivation is damage control; the protocols below are built so we rarely need it.
  • Our standard is simple: nobody leaves without a reason to come back.
  • Every patient walks out with their next two appointments booked.

Part 1 — The Never-Lose-Them Standard

This is the core rule. If we follow it, attrition drops before reactivation ever enters the picture.

The Rule
  • Every patient leaves with 2 appointments booked
  • At least 1 hygiene and 1 restorative
  • Unless they have completed all treatment
  • If they completed all treatment, offer 10% off a nightguard or whitening as a "thank you for trusting us with your care."
If They Resist
  • At least get them on the hygiene schedule
  • Place a note in the appointment for the hygienist that the patient still needs convincing/closing to commit to the restorative appointment
  • Tell the patient: "Absolutely! I'm going to follow up with you next Tuesday to answer any questions and get you taken care of."
  • No asking. No hesitation. We aren't doing them a favor — following up is our standard protocol for every patient.

How to Schedule SRP (Scaling & Root Planing) Patients

  1. Book both SRP appointments before they leave (instead of the standard hygiene + restorative pairing).
  2. After the first-side SRP, while they wait for the second side, schedule their 4-week re-evaluation. Now they have their second and third appointment on the books.
  3. After the second-side SRP, book their first restorative appointment. Now they have their third and fourth appointment booked. "Janice, now that we're on our way to getting your gums stable, Dr. Abedini really wants to address that lower right molar before it gets worse and the treatment changes."
  4. From that point forward, they always carry two future appointments: a perio maintenance visit and a restorative visit.
  5. The only exception: the patient has completed all planned treatment.

Prophy (Routine Hygiene) Patients

  • Always schedule 1 hygiene appointment and 1 restorative appointment before they leave.
  • The only exception: the patient has completed all planned treatment.

Patients Who Have Completed Treatment

  • Thank them by name for "trusting us with your care — your trust means so much to us."
  • Offer 10% off a nightguard or whitening as a special gift for completing their treatment.
  • Still book their next hygiene appointment — completed treatment does not mean completed relationship.

Part 2 — The Follow-Up Cadence

When a patient has outstanding treatment and hasn't booked, or misses/cancels, we follow a fixed contact rhythm. Consistency is the whole point — no patient slips because someone forgot.

Log every touch in the patient's file. If they book at any point, stop the cadence. If they ask to stop being contacted, honor it and note it.

Tips:

  1. Warm, specific, doctor-driven reason.
  2. Be specific. Review (1) chart, (2) photos, (3) notes and explain why this is serious.
  3. Offer two specific time slots.
  4. In your mind, assume you're closing the deal — talk that way instead of being unsure/hesitant.
  5. After months of no contact, hold off until they come in for their next hygiene appointment.

Part 3 — The Scripts

These are built on real persuasion principles, not pressure. Every script does three things: names something specific to THIS patient, frames the doctor as personally concerned about them, and closes assumptively with two time options instead of asking yes or no. Personalize the name and the tooth/treatment — personalization pierces through the spam feeling.

The Psychology Toolkit We're Using

  • Personalization — naming the exact tooth/finding makes it impossible to feel like a mass text.
  • Authority transfer — "Dr. Abedini asked me to reach out" carries the doctor's weight, not the front desk's.
  • Loss aversion — patients act harder to avoid losing something (a simpler/cheaper fix) than to gain something.
  • Assumptive close — offering two slots skips the "do you want to come in" yes/no and moves straight to "which works."
  • Specific, near-term windows — "Wednesday morning or Thursday afternoon" beats "sometime soon" every time.

Restorative Reactivation Scripts

For patients with outstanding restorative treatment (fillings, crowns, etc.) who haven't booked or have lapsed.

1. The Doctor's Personal Concern
"Hi [Name], it's Tabetha at Love Dental. Dr. Abedini asked me to reach out because he's been thinking about your [upper-right silver filling]. Right now it's a straightforward fix with a [crown], but he's concerned if it keeps going it won't be as easy or cheap to fix and might need [a root canal]. He told me to find you time this week. I can get you in Wednesday morning or Thursday afternoon. Which one do you prefer?"
Why it works: Authority transfer + loss aversion (simple now vs. bigger later) + assumptive two-slot close. Stakes made concrete instead of vague.
2. The Window-Closing Angle
"Hi [Name], Tabetha from Love Dental. Quick heads-up on your cavities. You have a few at various stages and most are right at the point where a [filling] still solves it. Dr. Abedini is most concerned about the ones on your lower left and says waiting much longer and that same tooth usually needs a [crown/root canal]. I'm working on finding availabilities for our most urgent patients now, so I've got Tuesday at 10 or Friday at 2 available for you — if you tell me which is better I'll save that spot."
Why it works: Loss aversion made financial (small fix vs. expensive fix) — the strongest motivator for restorative. Specific slots close it.
3. The Urgency Nudge
"Hi [Name], it's Tabetha at Love Dental. Dr. Abedini discussed your treatment with me this morning and is particularly concerned about the lower left molar. He said the fracture is manageable now but fractures are unpredictable and will break without notice at the most random moment — then we're having an implant conversation. He wants me to get you in for a crown as soon as possible. Can you make it next Monday at 10 or Wednesday at 11?"
Why it works: Commitment & consistency — people hate abandoning something they've already invested in. Frames finishing as protecting past effort.
4. The Urgency + Scarcity Nudge
"Hi [Name], it's Tabetha at Love Dental. Dr. Abedini discussed your treatment with me this morning and is particularly concerned about the [lower left molar]. He said the [fracture is manageable now but fractures are unpredictable and will break without notice at the most random moment — then we're having an implant conversation]. He wants me to get you in for a crown as soon as possible but we're booked this week. Can you make it next Monday at 10 or Wednesday at 11?"
Why it works: Commitment & consistency + honest scarcity (genuinely booked this week) raises the value of the slot offered.
5. The Insurance-Timing Hook
"Hi [Name], Tabetha from Love Dental. I'm reviewing your account and realized you've still got insurance benefits available this year for the [crown you need for the big cavity on your upper right]. Those benefits reset and disappear at year-end. It'd be a shame to leave that on the table when the tooth needs the work anyway. I remember you told me you like mornings — I can get you in Monday at 10am or Thursday at 8am."
Why it works: Loss aversion tied to expiring benefits + a real deadline. Works hardest in Q4. Honest, not manufactured urgency.
6. The Symptom Check-In
"Hi [Name], it's Tabetha at Love Dental. Dr. Abedini wanted me to follow up and check if your tooth has been giving you any sensitivity or discomfort yet. Those types of cavities tend to spread and become more uncomfortable the longer you wait. He wants us to be ahead of it and asked me to work with your schedule to get you in next week. Next week I have a Tuesday at 9:30am or a Thursday 1:30pm. Which one do you prefer?"
Why it works: Opens a two-way conversation instead of a pitch. Self-diagnosis pulls them in; if they report a symptom, they close themselves.
7. The Short-and-Direct (for busy patients)
"Hi [Name], it's Tabetha at Love Dental. Dr. Abedini wants me to follow up with you about the fractured tooth on the lower right before it gets worse and the treatment changes. I want to get you in Wednesday at 1 or Thursday at 9. I'm working on scheduling our most urgent patients so let me know which one is better and I'll save that for you now."
Why it works: Respects the patient who hates long texts. Low friction, two slots, you do the work. Great for high-earners/professionals.
8. The Re-Care After a No-Show
"Hi [Name], it's Tabetha at Love Dental — we missed you for the [fillings you need on the upper left] and totally get that life gets busy. We always joke that bacteria doesn't understand our daily struggle, but we do and we care! Dr. Abedini wants to fix it before it progresses. I saved you priority booking: Tuesday at 8am or Wednesday at 2pm. Which works better this time?"
Why it works: Removes shame (reduces avoidance), keeps the door warm, reasserts the clinical reason, and offers a fresh assumptive close.
9. Urgency but Quick
"Hi [First], it's Love Dental. Dr. [Doctor] had recommended [treatment, plain language — e.g. 'a crown on one of your back teeth'] at your last visit. We'd love to get you taken care of before it turns into something bigger. I only have an opening Monday at 1:30p or Thursday at 8am. Which one works with your schedule?"
Why it works: Keeps the door warm, reasserts the clinical reason, and offers a fresh assumptive close with honest scarcity.
10. "How Much Will It Cost?"
"Great question. It depends on your insurance, and based on what they're estimating it looks like they're covering [XXXX] while your portion is the remaining [XXXX]. Good news is I'm able to split your portion into easy monthly payments now. I can save Thursday at 1:30p for you, and if you come in 15 minutes early we can play around with the calculator to see which payment plan is your favorite."
Why it works: Answers cost without sticker shock, reframes to monthly, and closes with a specific slot plus a low-pressure reason to arrive early.

Hygiene Reactivation Scripts

For patients overdue for a cleaning, perio maintenance, or who've drifted off recall.

1. The Perio Maintenance Reminder
"Hi [Name], it's Tabetha at Love Dental. Because of the gum treatment you completed, Kayla recommended a 3-month maintenance cycle. Staying on schedule is the single biggest thing protecting the investment you already made from needing another deep cleaning. I can reserve your professional cleaning for Monday at 8am or Wednesday at 2:30pm. Which one is better?"
Why it works: Ties the visit to protecting an investment they already made. Perio patients drift fastest; this reframes maintenance as defense, not optional.
2. The "It's Been a While" Warm Reach
"Hi [Name], it's Tabetha from Love Dental — we've missed seeing you! It's been about [X months] since your last cleaning, and we want to make sure nothing's quietly building up between visits. That's how a routine professional cleaning becomes a deep cleaning later. I can reserve this Thursday at 8am or Monday at 1pm for you — let me know which is better."
Why it works: Warm + early-detection framing (loss aversion, gentle). The personal "we've missed you" rebuilds the relationship before the ask.
3. The New-Year / Benefits Reset
"Hi [Name], it's Tabetha at Love Dental. Your dental benefits just reset, which means your cleaning is likely covered at 100%! I'd love to get you in before the calendar fills up this time of year. I can reserve this Thursday at 8am or Monday at 1pm for you — let me know which is better."
Why it works: Anchors on a covered, easy 'yes' visit. Low-stakes entry point that gets lapsed patients back through the door and re-engaged.
4. The Family-Anchor Script
"Hi [Name], it's Tabetha from Love Dental. I was booking [family member]'s cleaning and noticed you're overdue for yours too. Want me to get you both in around the same time so it's one trip? I have [Wednesday June 15 at 10am] with two openings back-to-back."
Why it works: Convenience + social anchoring. Bundling with a family member already booked makes saying yes nearly automatic.
5. The Health-Connection Angle
"Hi [Name], it's Tabetha at Love Dental. I'm reaching out because we're behind on your professional cleaning, and since gum health is tied to things like heart health and blood sugar, Dr. Abedini likes to keep our patients on track with their health. I can reserve this Thursday at 8am or Monday at 1pm for you — let me know which is better."
Why it works: Elevates a cleaning from cosmetic to whole-body health. Raises perceived stakes for patients who deprioritize 'just a cleaning.'
6. The Comeback (long-lapsed, 12+ months)
"Hi [Name], [Your Name] at Love Dental. It's been a while and we'd genuinely love to have you back — no lecture, promise. Things may have changed since your last visit, so Dr. Abedini suggests a fresh check and cleaning to reset the baseline. First step's easy. Can I offer you [day] or [day]?"
Why it works: Disarms the guilt that keeps long-lapsed patients away ('no lecture, promise'). Lowers the barrier to a single easy first step.
7. The Slot-Saver (uses scarcity honestly)
"Hi [Name], it's [Your Name] at Love Dental. We had a [day] [morning] hygiene slot open up — I thought of you since you're due. These tend to go fast. Want me to hold it for you? If that time doesn't work I've also got [day] at [time]."
Why it works: Honest scarcity (a real opening) + you-came-to-mind personalization. The 'hold it for you' framing creates gentle, genuine urgency.

Part 4 — Rules for the Team

  1. Always personalize the brackets. A script with [tooth #] left blank is worse than no text. If you don't know the specific finding, pull the chart before you send.
  2. Never send more than one script style per cadence step. Rotate angles across touches so it never feels like a robot.
  3. Two specific time slots beats "when are you free?" every single time. Always close with options.
  4. Match the patient. Busy professional → short script. Anxious/long-lapsed → warm, no-pressure script.
  5. Log every contact and every outcome. We track which scripts actually book so we can double down on winners.
  6. The moment they book, stop the cadence and confirm warmly. The moment they opt out, honor it.

Part 5 — How to Contact Patients

  1. Texts have a ceiling. They're great at "Hey, you're due, want to grab a time?" and terrible at re-selling someone who ghosted on $4K of diagnosed treatment. That patient needs a human who can handle "I've been meaning to…" and book them on the spot. The higher the value and the longer the lapse, the more a call earns its keep.
  2. Tier 1 — Run text-first on the easy tier (lapsed under 9 months, overdue recare, no big open treatment): Text first. If no reply in 3 days, call. If no answer, leave a voicemail and a follow-up text referencing the voicemail. The combination outperforms either alone because you're hitting the channel each patient actually uses.
  3. Tier 2 — Make calls for the high-value tier (unscheduled treatment on the books, 9+ months lapsed, patients who specifically said "I'll think about it"). These are worth a human's time because the production per conversion is high and a text won't close them. Call, then text as backup.
  4. Personalize the text: name + specific reason + a specific date/time.
  5. Keep it conversational, not a wall of text. One ask per message.
  6. When offering dates, suggest the same date/time as the patient's last appointment.
  7. Three touches across two channels. After that, stop chasing — diminishing returns, and you don't want to feel like spam. Place on a long-cycle list and re-touch in 60–90 days.
Kal's Coaching Note

Reactivation is the most expensive way to fill a schedule — every patient on this list is one Part 1 failed to hold. The real win is the two-appointment standard, not the rescue scripts. If your team nails "nobody leaves without their next two booked," the reactivation list shrinks until it's a handful of genuinely lapsed patients instead of a leaking bucket you bail out every month. Use the scripts, but track which step of Part 1 broke for every patient who landed here — that's where the actual money is.
