The New Patient Experience

Patient Care Coordinator Flow
The patient is anxious. Your job is to make them feel informed, led, and trusted. Three rules run through every line below:
  1. State, don't ask. Lead them warmly instead of asking permission.
  2. One question at a time. Ask, then go quiet and listen.
  3. Stay in your lane. Dr. Abedini does diagnosis and trust. You do the cost conversation and the close.
1
The Greeting  — you walk out to the waiting area
Say
"Hi [Patient Name] — I'm Tabetha. I work right alongside Dr. Abedini, and I'll be with you through your whole visit today. I'm really glad you're here. Come on back with me — I'll get you settled."
Why it works
  • You — not the assistant — being the first face sets the entire relationship. This is the most important 10 seconds of the visit.
  • No "Can I walk you back?" You're leading, not asking. An anxious patient wants to be led.
  • Reduce the number of questions they're asked. They already field 5–10 admin questions at check-in, 5–10 clinical questions in the operatory, and more from the doctor. We still need them to say yes to treatment — so we don't want the most important answer they give to be the 25th one.
2
Getting to Know the Patient on a Personal Level  — after and/or during the tour
After or during the tour, ask a personal question or two to get to know them.
Use something genuine as a starting point — something they mentioned on the intake form, another state on their insurance card or license that hints they moved here, something they're holding or wearing. Use what comes naturally.
Why it works
  • Show them we care about them on a personal level.
  • This becomes another anchor the doctor can use to connect with the patient.
  • Input interesting details into the patient's chart and let the doctor know.
3
First Discovery  — walking back to the operatory
Say
"So tell me — what made you decide to come in today?"
Then stop talking. Let them answer fully. Don't finish their sentences.
Why it works
  • One question. Their answer is your anchor for the whole appointment — you'll quote it back during the case presentation.
  • If they mention anxiety, name it: "A lot of people feel exactly that way. We'll go slow, and I'll check in with you the whole time."
  • Write down their exact words.
4
Surfacing Barriers  — seated, before the assistant sets up
Say
"Before we get started, I want a couple of minutes with you. Be honest with me — has anything held you back from getting dental work done before? For most people it's one thing in particular."
Then quiet. Let it land.
Why it works
  • One question. Cost, time, and past bad experiences surface on their own — you don't need to list them out loud.
  • Surfacing it now means you're never ambushed by it later during case presentation.
  • If finances come up early, normalize it: "We've got a lot of ways to make treatment work — we'll go through all of that together."
5
The "One Thing" Question  — your close-out before handoff
Say
"Last thing before I let you get your photos taken — if we could fix just one thing for you, what would it be?"
Why it works
  • The single most valuable sentence you'll collect. It becomes your exact opener when you return for case presentation.
  • You only need 3–4 minutes total for Stages 2–4. Two real questions and genuine eye contact is plenty.
6
Handoff to the Assistant
PCC
"I'm going to have [DA Name] take some photos and x-rays so Dr. Abedini gets a really thorough look. You're in great hands — I'll be back once he's had a chance to go over everything."
Assistant
"I'm going to take a full set of x-rays and some photos of your smile so Dr. Abedini gets a complete picture. About 20 minutes — nice and easy."
Why it works
  • Everything loaded and charted before Dr. Abedini walks in. No waiting after intake.
  • Assistant flags anything notable to PCC before the huddle.
7
The Huddle  — brief Doctor before he enters
PCC → Doctor
"[Patient Name] came in because [their exact words]. They mentioned [any barrier — cost, anxiety, past experience]. Their number-one goal is [the 'one thing' from Stage 5]. They seem [engaged / nervous / motivated]."
Why it works
  • This 60–90 seconds is the highest-leverage moment in the entire flow. Never skip it.
  • It completely changes how the doctor walks into the room.
  • If he's running behind, pass it as a quick note or text.
8
Doctor Enters  — anchored to their concern
Doctor
"[Patient Name], Tabetha told me you've been thinking about [their stated concern] — I want to take a close look at that today. Let's go through it together."
During photo review
"What I'm seeing here is [findings in plain language]. This connects directly to what you mentioned about [their concern]."
Why it works
  • Target 15–20 minutes. Doctor's job is diagnosis and trust — not the money conversation.
  • The assistant enters findings into the plan in real time, as he speaks.
  • Anchoring to their concern first shows the whole team listened.
9
Doctor Exits Cleanly  — no fee talk
Doctor
"I'm going to put together everything I recommend, and Tabetha's going to walk you through all of it — the what, the why, and how we make it work for you. She's the best there is at this. I'll check back before you leave if you have more clinical questions."
Why it works
  • Doctor does NOT discuss fees, phasing, or payment. That is PCC's lane, every time.
  • If he tends to linger, agree on a quiet signal beforehand — eye contact, a sticky note, or Open Dental messaging.
10
PCC Returns & Resets the Room
Say
"[Patient Name], how are you doing? Let me sit with you for a few minutes and walk through everything."
Why it works
  • Have the assistant step out. Dim the overhead light. Sit at eye level. Sit next to the patient, not across from them.
  • You're resetting from "clinical exam" to "two people figuring this out together."
11
Present, Anchored to Their Words  — never open with the fee
Say
"When you came in, you told me [their exact words]. Everything Doctor is recommending connects right back to that. Let me show you what he found. His plan focuses on fixing it for you and — most importantly — preventing it from happening again to that tooth and the others."
Walk through in plain language
"There are a few things he wants to take care of. The most important one for you right now is [priority tied to their concern]. Here's what it looks like [show photo, not x-ray]. And here's what it's actually like to have it done — '2 appointments: one to fix the problem and get the tooth ready for the lab, and another much quicker visit to deliver the crown. Dr. Abedini knows most patients don't enjoy the numbing part, so he recommends taking care of the fillings near that tooth at the same appointment. That's it! Easy peezy.'"
Why it works
  • Use a printed or iPad one-pager in plain language — something they can hold. Holding it reduces fee shock.
  • Never say "treatment plan." Say "what we recommend" or "what Doctor wants for you."
12
The Cost Conversation  — with confidence
If treatment is under $10,000
"Dr. Abedini recommended a personalized plan to get you from where we are now to your healthiest self. Your plan takes care of [that broken tooth] plus the rest of what you need. I'll review everything, but because you said [XXXX] is your primary concern, Dr. Abedini would like to get started on that first. Here's what we're looking at. Your insurance is incredible! Between what your insurance is helping with and what Dr. Abedini wanted to do for you personally, your portion is only [XXXX]. Would you like to take care of that in one piece, or separate it into monthly payments?"
If treatment is over $10,000
"Dr. Abedini recommended a personalized plan to get you from where we are now to your healthiest self. Your plan takes care of [their primary concern] plus the rest of what you need. Because you said [XXXX] is your primary concern, Dr. Abedini would like to get started on that first. Here's what we're looking at. Your insurance is incredible! Between what your insurance is helping with and what Dr. Abedini wanted to do for you personally, your portion is only [XXXX]. Would you like to take care of that in one piece, or separate it into monthly payments?"
Then PAUSE. Wait. Do not fill the silence.
Why it works
  • Silence after the number is normal. Resist filling it. The first person to talk after the number usually loses.
  • If they don't commit, ask the diagnostic question in 12c: "Is it the total amount, or is it more about the timing?" Their answer tells you exactly which solution to offer.
12b
If the full plan is over $10,000 and they ask to see everything
Do not provide the entire plan unless they are adamant. A full total ignores insurance and phasing — it's an inaccurate, scary number that shuts patients down.
Counter with
"I totally get wanting the full picture — that's fair. Here's the honest reason I don't want to just hand you a big total today: that number wouldn't be accurate, and I'd rather not give you a wrong number. It doesn't account for your insurance, and it assumes everything gets done at once, which is almost never how we actually do it. What I can do is show you the full picture in phases — what we tackle first, what it actually costs after your insurance, and what comes later. That way every number you see is real. Sound fair?"
If they push again
  • Don't fight it. Give a range with a strong caveat: "If you want a ballpark, everything together could land anywhere from [X] to [Y] depending on insurance and how we phase it — but that's exactly why I don't lead with it, because the real number is almost always lower and it depends on choices we make together. Let's start with what's actually accurate: phase one."
12c
The Financing Fork  — after you ask "total, or timing?"
They didn't commit, so you asked: "Is it the total amount, or is it more about the timing?" The answer routes everything that follows. Match the solution to the answer.

"It's the total"

The size of the number is the problem. Shrink what they're committing to right now.
1. Phase the treatment"We don't have to do all of this at once — almost nobody does. Let's take care of [the priority] first, and we space the rest out as it makes sense for you."
2. Maximize insurance across years"Your benefits reset in January. If we do phase one now and phase two after the reset, you get two years of insurance instead of one."
3. Separate urgent from elective"Here's what truly needs attention now, and here's what we can keep an eye on." Shrinks the must-do number honestly.

"It's the timing"

They believe in the treatment — they just can't write the check this month. Spread the same number over time.
1. Cherry (lead here)"We work with Cherry, which lets you break this into monthly payments — and it's just a soft check, so seeing your options won't affect your credit at all. Want me to pull up what your monthly would look like? Takes about two minutes."
2. Sunbit (fallback)If Cherry doesn't approve or doesn't cover the full amount: "Let me check one more — we also work with Sunbit, which tends to have a little more flexibility. Give me one second."
3. Schedule around their cash flow"Let's book phase one for [date that works] and lock today's pricing." Holds the case without forcing the money now.
How to run the financing offer
  • Frame the soft check every time. The #1 reason patients won't even look at financing is fear it'll ding their credit. "Soft check, won't affect your credit" up front removes the objection before they raise it.
  • Show the monthly, not the total. Once you're in Cherry, the number they should see is the monthly payment — that's the whole point of a timing solution.
  • Don't stack all the options at once. Offer Cherry, let it run, reach for Sunbit only if Cherry comes up short. Listing every option makes it sound like you're trying hard to get them into debt.
The trap — match the solution to the answer

If it's a timing problem and you start phasing the treatment, you just shrank a number that wasn't the obstacle — and may have under-treated someone who'd have financed the whole thing. If it's a total problem and you push Cherry, you're asking someone already scared of the number to take on debt for it — that backfires. The diagnostic question only helps if the solution matches the answer.

13
The Close
If they're ready
"It sounds like [priority item] is the one you want to take care of first. Let's get that on the schedule while you're here — does that work?"
If they need time
"Totally understand. I'll put together a summary of everything we talked about so you've got it in writing. I'll follow up with you in a couple of days — does that work?"
Why it works
  • End every appointment with a clear next step — even if it's just a scheduled follow-up call.
  • A booked follow-up is a win. An open-ended "let me think about it" with no date is not.
Total appointment window: 60–75 minutes
