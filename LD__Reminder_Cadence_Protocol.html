Reminder & Engagement Cadence

The schedule of touches that gets patients in the chair, and the words for each one

Team Operating Standard · Front Office & Hygiene · Owner: Dr. Abedini · Built for Flex Dental automation · Internal document

This is the pre-appointment sequence: every text, email, and call we send from the moment an appointment is booked until the patient is in the chair. A missing reminder is the single most common reason patients don't show. But a reminder is a chance to reinforce a commitment, never to reopen the decision. Once they're here, the Cancellation Prevention Protocol takes over.

The one idea that runs through all of it

We do not "confirm." We remind, warmly and factually, and we speak as if showing up is simply what happens. The word "confirm" trains patients to think appointments here are loose and breakable. Our software still tracks status underneath (see Section 2), but the patient never hears that word.

1Why the Cadence Scales by Lead Time

Most of our production is near-term, booked one to three days out. Those appointments don't need a long sequence; there's no time for one, and over-texting a patient who's coming tomorrow just annoys them. A smaller set of appointments sit a week or two out, and that gap is where no-shows breed, because life has time to get in the way.

So one fixed cadence is the wrong tool. We run three tiers gated by how far out the appointment is. Short bookings get a light sequence. Far-out bookings get the full treatment. Flex applies the right tier automatically based on lead time.

TierBookedTouchesChannels

Two rules that apply to every tier

1. Once a patient actively responds, the redundant follow-ups stop. Flex suppresses the rest of the sequence automatically so we never over-message someone who already engaged.

2. Every touch is a reminder, never a "confirm." The wording below already reflects this. Don't reintroduce the word.

2Confirm vs. Remind: the Language and the Software

These are two separate things, and we treat them separately:

  • What the patient reads (the message): no "confirm." Factual, warm, "we're looking forward to seeing you, reply Y so we know you got this."
  • What the software records (status color): we absolutely still track this. It's the schedule's vital signs.

Important: a button tap is the weakest commitment there is

If a patient taps "Y" but still no-shows, that green status was a false signal. People tap confirm on autopilot without reading. So we do not treat an auto-tap as our highest-confidence tier. The strongest green is a patient who got a live voice touch and said "yes, I'll be there," or who made a verbal commitment / pinky promise at booking. Status should track commitment quality, not just whether a button got pressed.

Our internal status tiers (redefined)

StatusWhat earns itConfidence
Verbally CommittedSpoke to patient live (call or in person); they committed. Or strong verbal commitment / pinky promise at booking.Highest
Text ConfirmedPatient tapped Y / replied to a reminder. Real, but weak.Medium
Reminder Sent, No ReplyTouches went out, no response yet.Low, treat as at-risk

If Flex/Open Dental let you create or rename status categories, build these two distinct buckets ("Verbally Committed" vs "Text Confirmed") so the team manages to the real signal. A near-term appointment sitting at "Reminder Sent, No Reply" the day before is a call, not a shrug.

3Tier A · Near-Term (booked 1 to 3 days out)

Tight bookings. Two clean touches, both text. No email, no call, unless the patient is flagged high-risk (Section 6).

Tier A Sequence

2 touches, text only

TextDay before

Sent afternoon/early evening the day prior.

Text"Hi [First], it's Love Dental! We're all set and looking forward to seeing you tomorrow at [time] with Dr. Abedini. We've got everything ready for you. Reply Y so we know you got this. See you then!"

TextMorning of

Sent 2 to 3 hours before the appointment.

Text"Good morning [First]! Just a friendly note that we're ready for you at [time] today. Looking forward to seeing you! If anything's changed, give us a quick call at [number]."
Why this works Two warm, factual touches assume attendance and give no "confirm" language to push against. The morning-of text catches the day-of forgetters, the most common near-term no-show.

New patients: the Welcome Call (one human touch, not two)

Two days before a new patient's first visit (or at the time of booking if the appointment is sooner than that), the front desk calls personally to welcome them. Two days out is deliberate: it's far enough ahead that if the patient surfaces a problem on the call (a work conflict, childcare, cold feet), we still have room to solve it and save the slot, rather than catching it too late the day before. This is the single highest-ROI touch we have for new patients, and it's the only live welcome call they get. New patients still get the normal automated texts and booking email; the welcome call is their one human touch. We keep it warm and personal because that relationship-building is what gets a new patient in the door and turns them into a long-term patient.

Welcome call"Hi Ms. Jones, this is [Name] at Love Dental! We're so looking forward to meeting you on [day] at [time]. We'll have everything ready for you. Is there anything we can do ahead of time to make your first visit more comfortable? ... Wonderful, we'll see you [day] at [time]. We can't wait to meet you!"

If no answer: leave a genuine voicemail in the moment (a real "I tried you live" message still carries warmth). Never a pre-recorded or ringless drop, which lands like a robocall and defeats the personal touch.

Why this works Authority transfer plus reciprocity. A patient the doctor personally welcomed feels seen and is far more likely to show.

6When to Add a Live Call

Calls do not scale across every patient, and they shouldn't. Automation (text + email) carries the load for everyone. We reserve live calls for the roughly 15 to 20% of appointments where a human voice actually changes the outcome:

Add a call when...Why
High-value appointment (large production: crowns, multi-unit, surgery, big restorative)The dollar loss from a no-show justifies the labor every time.
High-risk patient (previously no-showed or canceled late, repeat offender, on the VIP list)History predicts behavior. These need the strongest commitment we can get.
Far-out booking (the appointment has sat 10+ days)The longer the gap, the weaker the original commitment has gotten.
New patient (the front desk welcome call)New-patient no-shows are the most expensive kind.

The goal of the call

Get a real verbal commitment and mark them Verbally Committed (the strongest green). The call is not just a reminder, it's how we upgrade a weak text-tap into a commitment that actually predicts showing up.

8Setting It Up in Flex

  1. Build three rule sets gated by lead time: 1 to 3 days (Tier A), 4 to 10 days (Tier B), 10+ days (Tier C). Flex assigns the tier based on how far out the appointment is booked.
  2. Set each touch to the channel and timing in Sections 3 to 5. Paste the matching verbiage.
  3. Turn on response suppression so that once a patient replies/taps, the remaining touches in their sequence stop firing. (This is the "if they hit a button, they stop getting them" behavior.)
  4. Keep status tracking on, but make sure the team knows a text-tap is "Text Confirmed," not "Verbally Committed." Only a live touch earns the top tier.
  5. Flag high-value / high-risk / new patients for the live call layer (Section 6). This is a human step, not automated, so build it into the daily front-desk routine: each morning, pull tomorrow's high-value and high-risk appointments and assign the calls.
  6. Never reintroduce the word "confirm" into any template, even if Flex's default templates use it. Replace them.

Don't go dark

Whatever you turn on or off, keep at least the day-before and morning-of touches live for everyone. Missing reminders is the number-one cause of no-shows, cutting reminders to "fix" the confirm-language problem would raise no-shows, not lower them. We're changing the words, not removing the touch.
